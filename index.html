🇮🇪 English (UK / Ireland)

Before anything else

Anonymise every transcript: replace participant names and any identifying details with codes (P1, P2, etc.). Save each file as plain text. Read each transcript through once before coding. Do not skip this.

Step 1 — Label your interview questions

Assign a label to each question in your interview guide: Q1, Q2, Q3, and so on. Keep a reference document with each Q label and its full question text. These become your matrix column headers and a dissertation appendix.

Step 2 — Initial coding with Prompt 1

Open ChatGPT, Claude (claude.ai), or Google Gemini. Copy Prompt 1 from Section 6 of this guide. Fill in your research topic and list your interview questions. Paste the full anonymised transcript at the bottom. Run it once per participant. Save each output (e.g. P1_codes.txt).

Step 3 — Build your codebook with Prompt 2

Once you have coded all transcripts, copy all Prompt 1 outputs together and paste them into Prompt 2. This produces a unified codebook. Review every code. Correct anything that does not accurately reflect your data.

Step 4 — Final coding with Prompt 3

Paste your codebook and your interview questions into Prompt 3. Add the transcript. Run it once per participant. This gives you the clean, consistent row of codes for each participant's row in the matrix.

Step 5 — Build the matrix in Excel

Rows = participants (P1, P2...). Columns = questions (Q1, Q2...). Enter the codes from your Prompt 3 outputs. Use a dash where no code applies. Apply Conditional Formatting to colour-code by code. Build a legend below the matrix. Export as an image for your dissertation.

Step 6 — Analyse and write up

Count code frequencies (COUNTIF in Excel). Identify dominant themes and outliers. For each major theme: name it, quote a participant directly, and connect it to your literature review. In your methodology chapter: include a reflexivity statement, address trustworthiness (Lincoln and Guba, 1985), justify your sample size using data saturation, and acknowledge AI assistance transparently.

What is Qualitative Research?

Before you can understand qualitative coding analysis, you need to understand the type of research it belongs to. Research in the social sciences broadly divides into two camps: quantitative and qualitative.

Quantitative Research Qualitative Research
Deals with numbers and statistics Deals with words, meanings, and experiences
Asks "how many?" or "how much?" Asks "why?" or "how does this work?"
Large samples, surveys, experiments Small samples, interviews, observations
Results presented as tables and percentages Results presented as themes and narratives
Objective and measurable Interpretive and contextual

Your dissertation uses qualitative research. This means you are not trying to measure something with numbers; you are trying to understand something: the experiences, opinions, and perspectives of the people you interviewed.

The Philosophical Foundation

Qualitative research sits within an interpretivist philosophical tradition. This means the researcher believes that reality is constructed through human interpretation; different people experience the same situation differently, and understanding those differences is itself valuable knowledge (Creswell and Poth, 2018).

This is why qualitative researchers conduct interviews rather than send out questionnaires. A questionnaire gives you a tick-box answer. An interview gives you the story behind the answer.

📖
As Denzin and Lincoln (2018, p. 10) describe it, qualitative research involves "an interpretive, naturalistic approach to the world. This means that qualitative researchers study things in their natural settings, attempting to make sense of, or interpret, phenomena in terms of the meanings people bring to them."

Why This Matters for Your Dissertation

When you write your methodology chapter, you will need to justify your research design. Being able to explain that you used qualitative coding analysis because your research aims to understand meaning and experience, rather than count responses, demonstrates the methodological awareness that examiners expect. The analysis technique you use must be aligned with your research paradigm, and qualitative coding is firmly grounded in the interpretivist tradition.

Semi-Structured Interviews Explained

There are three main types of research interview: structured, semi-structured, and unstructured. Your interviews were semi-structured, which is by far the most common format in master's dissertation research.

Type What It Looks Like When Used
Structured Fixed questions in a fixed order, no deviation. Almost like a verbal survey. When strict comparability between respondents is essential
Semi-Structured Prepared questions as a guide, but the conversation can explore interesting points further. Follow-up questions are allowed. Most dissertation research. Balances comparability with depth.
Unstructured A topic is introduced, but the conversation flows naturally, more like an informal discussion. Exploratory research, oral histories, ethnography

What Makes Semi-Structured Interviews Valuable

The semi-structured format gives you the best of both worlds. You have a consistent set of questions that all participants answered, which means you can compare responses across your sample. But you also had the freedom to probe deeper when a respondent said something interesting, which means your data is richer and more nuanced than a simple survey would produce.

As Bryman (2016, p. 470) explains, the semi-structured interview "is flexible; questions can be added or probed and the sequence of questions can be altered. This makes them much more useful than standardised interviews for generating a detailed understanding of social phenomena."

From Teams Call to Text File

Your interviews were conducted over Microsoft Teams, which automatically produces a transcript: a verbatim (word-for-word) record of everything said during the call, including timestamps and speaker labels. This transcript is your raw data.

⚠️
A note on transcript quality: MS Teams transcripts are generated by speech recognition software and are not always perfectly accurate. Before you begin coding, read through each transcript and correct any obvious errors, particularly where key terms in your research topic may have been mis-transcribed. This process is called data cleaning and should be mentioned in your methodology chapter.

Confidentiality and Anonymisation

Before you do anything else with your transcripts, ensure all participant names have been replaced with codes (P1, P2, P3...) and any identifying information has been removed. This is an ethical requirement and was part of your institutional ethical approval. Never submit transcripts to any AI tool with real names or identifying details.

✓
File naming convention: Rename your transcript files as P1_transcript.txt, P2_transcript.txt, and so on. Create a folder structure: /QCA/transcripts/, /QCA/coding_outputs/, and /QCA/matrix/. This keeps everything organised from the start.

What is Qualitative Coding Analysis?

Qualitative coding analysis (QCA) is the process of systematically organising and interpreting qualitative data by assigning short labels called codes to passages of text that share a common meaning or theme.

Think of it this way: if 6 out of 8 of your participants mentioned "lack of time" as a challenge, coding helps you see and document that pattern. Instead of reading 8 transcripts and trying to hold everything in your head, coding transforms messy conversational data into a structured, visual, and analytically tractable format.

Where Does QCA Come From?

The theoretical roots of qualitative coding come from Grounded Theory, developed by Glaser and Strauss (1967). Their core insight was that theory should emerge from data rather than being imposed upon it. Coding was their method for making that emergence systematic and defensible.

📌
Terminology note: In the published literature, the abbreviation "QCA" also refers to Qualitative Comparative Analysis, a configurational Boolean method developed by Ragin (1987) that is entirely different from what this guide describes. When writing your methodology chapter, refer to your approach as qualitative thematic coding or matrix-based qualitative analysis rather than "QCA" to avoid confusion.

The most widely cited practical framework comes from Strauss and Corbin (1998), who described three progressive stages of coding:

1

Open Coding

You read through the data and attach a label (a code) to any passage that seems significant. At this stage, you are not trying to organise; you are simply labelling. You might end up with 50 to 100 different codes. This is normal and expected.

Example: A participant says "We had no budget for software tools at all." Code: BUDGET

2

Axial Coding

You group your open codes into broader categories. Similar codes are clustered together. The goal is to identify relationships between codes and reduce the total number to a manageable set of categories (typically 15 to 25 for a dissertation study, though the right number depends on your data).

Example: BUDGET + STAFF_COSTS + FUNDING_GAP all grouped into a category: FINANCIAL

3

Selective Coding

You identify the core category or central theme that connects all the others. This becomes the central finding of your analysis: the overarching "story" that your data is telling.

Example: FINANCIAL, TIME, and SKILLS barriers all connect to a core theme: BARRIERS_TO_ADOPTION

📚
A more recent and accessible framework is provided by Saldana (2016) in The Coding Manual for Qualitative Researchers. Saldana describes over 30 coding methods and is considered the definitive practical guide to qualitative coding. Chapters 1 and 4 are especially relevant to dissertation-level research.

Sample Size and Data Saturation

With 7 to 8 participants, a question that may arise in examination is: is this sample large enough? The answer depends on the concept of data saturation: the point at which new interviews stop producing new codes and themes. In grounded theory and most qualitative traditions, saturation is the criterion for stopping data collection, not a predetermined number.

Guest, Bunce, and Johnson (2006) found empirically that basic thematic saturation often occurs between 6 and 12 interviews for relatively homogeneous samples. With 7 to 8 participants who share a common context (for example, managers in a specific industry or sector), you can legitimately argue that your sample is appropriate for thematic saturation. State this explicitly in your methodology chapter rather than waiting to be asked by an examiner.

The Codebook

A codebook is a reference document that lists every code you are using, its abbreviated label, its full meaning, and a brief description of what it represents. Building a codebook is a critical step in QCA because it ensures consistency: every time you encounter a passage about client relationships, you assign the same code (CLIE), not a different label each time.

Your codebook also becomes a key methodological appendix in your dissertation. It demonstrates to the examiner that your analysis was systematic, transparent, and not simply impressionistic reading.

Trustworthiness and Intercoder Reliability

A question examiners consistently ask about qualitative coding is: how do you know your codes are accurate and consistent? In quantitative research, this is addressed through statistical reliability measures. In qualitative research, the equivalent concern is framed through trustworthiness, as outlined by Lincoln and Guba (1985).

CriterionWhat It MeansHow to Address It
Credibility Are findings an accurate representation of participants' perspectives? Member checking: share your themes with participants for validation; immersive reading before coding
Dependability Would another researcher following the same process reach similar conclusions? Include your codebook as an appendix; document every coding decision transparently
Confirmability Are findings shaped by the data rather than researcher bias? Write a reflexivity statement acknowledging your position and assumptions (see Section 8)
Transferability Could findings apply in other contexts? Describe your sample and context in detail so readers can judge applicability

For single-coder studies (where only you are doing the coding, which is standard at dissertation level), confirmability is the most important criterion: be transparent about your role, assumptions, and the decisions you made during coding. If you want to go further, ask your supervisor to code a small subset (2 to 3 questions from one transcript) and compare codes. Discuss any differences. This peer debriefing step significantly strengthens your methodology chapter.

Coding Approaches: Where This Method Sits

You may have heard of thematic analysis, popularised by Braun and Clarke (2006). Thematic analysis and qualitative coding are closely related; both identify patterns in qualitative data. The key difference is that the matrix approach you will produce creates a structured visual table allowing direct cross-participant comparison. Thematic analysis tends to produce a narrative description of themes rather than a matrix.

The participant-question matrix format is most precisely described by Framework Analysis, developed by Ritchie and Spencer (1994) at the UK National Centre for Social Research and later refined by Gale et al. (2013). Framework Analysis was designed specifically for applied research with small samples and produces exactly the kind of matrix you are building here. When writing your methodology chapter, you may describe your approach as: "a matrix-based qualitative coding method informed by the Framework Analysis tradition (Ritchie and Spencer, 1994) and grounded in the interpretivist paradigm."

Why QCA Matters for Your Dissertation

  • It transforms raw interview data into structured, citable findings
  • It demonstrates rigour and systematicity to your examiner
  • It makes patterns visible across your participant sample at a glance
  • It produces a visual artefact (the matrix) that can be included directly in your dissertation
  • It provides the analytical foundation for connecting your findings to existing theory

Your Step-by-Step Workflow

Here is the complete workflow from raw transcript to finished QCA matrix. It involves three LLM prompts (detailed in Section 6), which you can use with ChatGPT, Claude, or Google Gemini. The process is designed so that the AI does the initial extraction work, while you as the researcher retain final interpretive authority.

⚠️
Before you start: Ensure all transcripts have been anonymised (real names replaced with P1, P2, etc. and organisations replaced with Company A, Company B, etc.) and saved as plain text files. Never paste identifiable data into any AI tool.
1

Prepare and organise your transcripts

Rename your MS Teams transcript files as P1_transcript.txt, P2_transcript.txt, and so on. Create a folder called /QCA/ with three subfolders inside: /transcripts/, /coding_outputs/, and /matrix/.

Read each transcript once through before doing any coding. This "immersive reading" helps you understand the data as a whole before you analyse the parts. It is mentioned by Saldana (2016) as a recommended first step.

2

List your interview questions and assign Q labels

Write out your interview guide and label each main question Q1, Q2, Q3, and so on. These labels will become the column headers in your matrix. If you had sub-questions, you can group them under the same Q label or give them separate columns (e.g., Q3a, Q3b).

Keep a reference sheet with your Q labels and full question text. You will include this in your dissertation appendix alongside your codebook.

3

Run Prompt 1 on each transcript (initial code extraction)

Open your chosen AI tool. Paste Prompt 1 (from Section 6), fill in the context fields, and paste the full transcript at the bottom. Run it for each participant separately. Save each output as a text file in your /coding_outputs/ folder (e.g., P1_codes.txt).

Review each output before moving on. The AI may occasionally misinterpret a section or assign a code that does not fit. Note any concerns; you will address them in the next step.

4

Run Prompt 2 to build a unified codebook

After coding all transcripts, collect all the codes generated across all participants and paste them together into Prompt 2. This prompt instructs the AI to identify overlapping codes, resolve inconsistencies, and produce a final unified codebook of 15 to 25 codes with descriptions.

Review the codebook carefully. You have the final say. If two codes should stay separate, split them. If a code seems redundant, remove it. Your codebook must reflect your own interpretive judgment, not just the AI's output.

5

Run Prompt 3 on each transcript (final matrix coding)

Now that you have a unified codebook, run Prompt 3 on each transcript individually. This prompt instructs the AI to apply only the codes from your codebook, not to invent new ones. This produces a clean, consistent row of codes for each participant.

Using a fixed codebook ensures comparability across all participants, which is what makes the matrix analytically valid and defensible in your methodology chapter.

6

Build the matrix in Excel

Open Excel and create a new sheet called "QCA Matrix." Set up rows for each participant and columns for each question. Enter the codes from Prompt 3's outputs into each cell. Use a dash (‑) for any question with no codeable content. See Section 7 for detailed Excel instructions.

7

Apply colour coding and create the legend

Assign a consistent colour to each code in your codebook. Fill each cell in the matrix with its code's colour using Excel's Conditional Formatting (see Section 7). Below the matrix, create a legend table listing each code, its colour, and its meaning. Take a screenshot of the completed matrix for your dissertation.

Choose colours that are clearly distinct from each other. Aim for no more than 12 to 15 distinct colours to keep the chart readable.

8

Analyse patterns and write up your findings

With your matrix complete, identify the dominant themes, cross-participant patterns, and any outlier cases. These form the core of your findings chapter. See Section 8 for guidance on how to write about what you find.

6

The LLM Prompts

The following three prompts are designed to work with any major AI language model: ChatGPT (chat.openai.com), Claude (claude.ai), or Google Gemini (gemini.google.com). Copy each prompt, replace the highlighted fields (shown in yellow in the code blocks below), and paste your transcript or coding outputs where indicated.

⚠️
Privacy reminder: Always anonymise transcripts before pasting them into any AI tool. Replace participant names with P1, P2, etc. and remove all company names, locations, and any other details that could identify participants. Your institution's ethics approval requires this.

Prompt 1: Initial Code Extraction

Run this prompt once for each participant transcript. It extracts raw thematic codes from a single interview. You will run it 7 or 8 times in total (once per participant) and save each output.

You are helping a master's dissertation student conduct qualitative coding analysis on a semi-structured interview transcript. --- FILL IN THE FIELDS BELOW BEFORE RUNNING --- Research topic: [e.g., "The role of AI tools in customer service within Irish SMEs"] Participant ID: [e.g., P1] Interview date: [e.g., January 2026] INTERVIEW QUESTIONS (paste your full list with Q labels): Q1: [your question text] Q2: [your question text] Q3: [your question text] [... continue for all questions] --- WHAT IS A THEMATIC CODE? A thematic code is a short label (3 to 6 capital letters, often an abbreviation) that captures the main CONCEPT or THEME expressed by the interviewee in response to a question. It is not a direct quote; it is an interpretation of meaning. Examples: - "We had no budget for tools at all" → Code: BUDGET - "Our clients expect faster responses now" → Code: CLIE - "Training staff took months and was costly" → Code: CAP - "Government grants were not accessible to us" → Code: GOB CODING RULES 1. Assign 1 to 3 codes per interview question 2. Keep codes SHORT: 3 to 6 capital letters (ADM, PLAN, CLIE, TIME, TEC...) 3. Focus on the RESPONDENT's answers, not the questions themselves 4. Use "-" if a question was skipped, not answered, or not applicable 5. Use consistent labels: if the same theme appears across questions, use the same code OUTPUT FORMAT Return a table with three columns: | Question | One-sentence summary of answer | Code(s) | |----------|-------------------------------|---------| | Q1 | Respondent described... | ADM, PLAN | | Q2 | Not answered | - | TRANSCRIPT [PASTE YOUR FULL ANONYMISED TRANSCRIPT HERE - delete this line before pasting]

Prompt 2: Codebook Consolidation

Run this prompt once, after you have Prompt 1 output for all participants. Paste all the raw codes from all participants together. The result is your unified codebook.

You are helping a master's dissertation student build a unified CODEBOOK for their qualitative coding analysis. Research topic: [INSERT YOUR RESEARCH TOPIC] Number of participants coded: [e.g., 7] YOUR TASK Review all the raw codes listed below and: 1. Identify codes that mean the same thing and merge them (e.g., "ADMIN", "ADM", and "ADMINISTRATION" should become one code: ADM) 2. Identify codes that are too vague and clarify their meaning 3. Remove any codes that appeared only once across all participants and are not central to the research topic 4. Produce a final CODEBOOK (aim for 15 to 25 codes; the right number depends on your data) OUTPUT FORMAT Return a table with three columns: | CODE | FULL MEANING | DESCRIPTION | |------|-------------|-------------| | ADM | Administration | References to internal administrative processes, bureaucracy, and operational management | | PLAN | Planning | Strategic or operational planning, goal-setting, and future-oriented decision-making | | ... continue for all codes ... | RAW CODES FROM ALL PARTICIPANTS [PASTE ALL YOUR PROMPT 1 OUTPUTS HERE, labelled by participant, e.g.:] --- P1 --- [paste P1 code table] --- P2 --- [paste P2 code table] [... and so on for all participants]

Prompt 3: Final Matrix Coding (Using Your Codebook)

Run this prompt once for each participant, using the unified codebook from Prompt 2. This produces the final, consistent row of codes you will enter into your Excel matrix.

You are a qualitative research assistant. You have a CODEBOOK and an interview transcript. Your job is to apply the codebook to the transcript and produce one row for a QCA coding matrix. Participant ID: [e.g., P3] Research topic: [your topic] CODEBOOK [PASTE YOUR COMPLETE CODEBOOK FROM PROMPT 2 HERE] INTERVIEW QUESTIONS [List your questions with their Q labels, for example:] Q1: What is your role and how long have you been in this position? Q2: What are the main challenges your organisation currently faces? Q3: How does your organisation currently use technology in its operations? [... continue for all questions] INSTRUCTIONS For each interview question: 1. Find the relevant section of the transcript 2. Apply 1 to 3 codes from the CODEBOOK that best describe the respondent's answer 3. Use "-" if the question was not answered or not applicable 4. ONLY use codes from the codebook above. Do not invent new codes. OUTPUT FORMAT Return a simple two-column table: | Question | Code(s) | |----------|---------| | Q1 | ADM | | Q2 | PLAN, TIME | | Q3 | - | TRANSCRIPT [PASTE THIS PARTICIPANT'S FULL ANONYMISED TRANSCRIPT HERE - delete this line before pasting]
✓
Model-specific tips: Claude and Gemini can typically handle long transcripts in a single message. If you are using the free tier of ChatGPT and your transcript is very long, split it into two messages and tell the model: "This is Part 1 of the transcript. Wait for Part 2 before coding." Always start a fresh conversation for each new participant to avoid the model mixing up responses.

Reviewing and Correcting AI Output

The AI will not be perfectly accurate every time. It may assign a code that does not quite fit, or miss a theme that you know is important. This is normal. After each Prompt 3 output, compare the codes against your own reading of the transcript. Where the AI's code does not match your interpretation, correct it and make a brief note explaining why. This reflexive process is itself evidence of analytical rigour and is worth a sentence or two in your methodology chapter.

Building Your Matrix in Excel

Once you have the Prompt 3 output for all participants, you are ready to build the QCA matrix in Excel. This section walks you through the process step by step.

Setting Up the Spreadsheet

1

Create the column headers

In Row 1: leave cell A1 blank. In B1 type Q1, in C1 type Q2, and continue across the row for each interview question. If you have 10 questions, you will fill B1 through K1. You can also add a second row of headers with the question topics (e.g., "Main Challenges", "Technology Use") to make the chart more readable.

2

Add participant IDs in Column A

In A2 type P1, in A3 type P2, and continue down for each participant. If you have 8 participants, fill A2 through A9.

3

Enter the codes from your Prompt 3 outputs

Using each participant's Prompt 3 output, type each code into the corresponding cell. For example: if P1's answer to Q3 was coded as TEC, type TEC in cell D2. Use a dash (‑) for questions with no code. If a cell has two codes (e.g., ADM, PLAN), type both separated by a comma.

Work one participant at a time and cross-check against your Prompt 3 output as you go.

4

Freeze the top row and first column

Click on cell B2. Go to: View > Freeze Panes > Freeze Panes. This keeps your participant IDs and question headers visible as you scroll in either direction.

Applying Colour Coding with Conditional Formatting

Conditional formatting automatically fills each cell with the correct colour based on the code it contains. This is faster and more accurate than filling cells manually one by one.

1

Select all data cells

Click on cell B2 and drag to the last data cell in your matrix (bottom-right corner, excluding headers and the participant ID column in Column A).

2

Open Conditional Formatting

Go to: Home tab > Conditional Formatting > New Rule.

3

Set the rule for the first code

Select "Format only cells that contain." Set the condition to: Cell Value > equal to > type the code label (e.g., ADM). Click "Format," go to the Fill tab, choose your colour for ADM (e.g., red). Click OK twice to save the rule.

4

Repeat for every code in your codebook

Go to Conditional Formatting > Manage Rules. Click "New Rule" to add a rule for each code. Each code gets its own rule and its own distinct colour.

Assign clearly different colours to each code. Where two codes are related (e.g., ADM and PLAN both belong to an "Operations" category), you can use shades of the same colour family to hint at the relationship.

⚠️
Cells with two codes: Conditional formatting applies only one rule per cell (the first matching rule in the list). For cells with two codes (e.g., "ADM, PLAN"), the cell will take the colour of whichever code's rule appears first. For a dissertation matrix this is acceptable; note in your methodology that multi-code cells are coloured by the primary code.

Building the Legend

Leave two or three blank rows below the matrix, then create a legend table. Your legend should have two columns: Code (with the cell filled in the matching colour) and Meaning (the full description). Apply the same fill colours in the legend as in the matrix. This legend becomes part of your dissertation figure.

Exporting for Your Dissertation

  1. Select the matrix and legend area together
  2. Copy the selection (Ctrl + C on Windows, Cmd + C on Mac)
  3. Open your Word dissertation document and use Paste Special > Picture for the cleanest formatting
  4. Alternatively, use the Snipping Tool (Windows) or Screenshot (Mac: Shift + Cmd + 4) to capture the matrix as an image
✓
Caption requirement: In your dissertation, the matrix must have a figure caption (e.g., "Figure 4: Qualitative Coding Analysis Matrix") and must be referenced by number in the text of your findings chapter. The examiner expects the matrix to be discussed, not just presented.

Interpreting and Writing About Your Results

The matrix is not itself a finding. It is the evidence that supports your findings. The analytical work happens when you interpret what the patterns in the matrix mean in relation to your research questions.

Step 1: Count Code Frequencies

Count how many times each code appears across the entire matrix (all participants, all questions). You can use Excel's COUNTIF function for this. Add a frequency table to a separate sheet. The most frequently appearing codes represent the dominant themes in your data.

📊
Example: If ADM (Administration) appears 14 times across all participants and questions, while TEC (Technology) appears only 4 times, administration is a much stronger theme in your data. This quantitative observation supports a qualitative discussion about why administration features so prominently.

Step 2: Identify Cross-Participant Patterns

Look for codes that appear for most or all participants in the same column. If Q4 (External Factors) is coded ECO (Economy) for 7 out of 8 participants, that is a highly consistent finding. It tells you that economic conditions are a near-universal concern in your sample when discussing external factors.

Step 3: Identify Outliers and Divergences

Look for participants whose coding pattern is noticeably different from the others. Perhaps P5 has codes that no other participant has, or P3 has very few dashes while everyone else has many. Outliers are not problems: they are analytically interesting. A divergent case often reveals the boundary conditions of a theme, or points to a subgroup within your sample worth discussing.

Step 4: Connect Themes to Your Research Questions

Return to your research question or questions. Does the pattern of codes help you answer them? If your research question is "What are the main barriers to digital adoption in SMEs?" and the codes BUDGET, TIME, and CAP (Training) appear most frequently, those three codes are your answer, supported by the matrix as documented evidence.

Writing the Findings Chapter

When writing about your QCA findings, structure each theme as follows:

  1. Present the matrix as a figure and describe its structure in one or two sentences
  2. Name the key themes based on your frequency analysis and cross-participant patterns
  3. Support each theme with a direct quote from the relevant transcript
  4. Interpret what the theme means in the context of your research question
  5. Connect the theme to existing literature from your literature review (does it confirm, challenge, or extend what previous researchers found?)

Example: From Matrix to Written Finding

Matrix observation

The code ADM (Administration) appears in 6 of 7 participants' responses to Q2 (Main Challenges).

Written finding

"Administrative burden emerged as the most consistently cited challenge across the participant sample, with six of seven participants referencing it when asked about their main operational difficulties (see Figure 4, Q2). As P3 noted: 'We spend so much time on paperwork and compliance that there is very little left for actual strategy.' This aligns with Bryman's (2016) observation that administrative demands disproportionately constrain smaller organisations, limiting their capacity for strategic innovation. The prevalence of this theme across diverse organisational contexts suggests it is a systemic rather than firm-specific challenge."

✓
Quality indicator: A strong findings chapter presents the matrix, names the key themes with frequency evidence, supports each theme with at least one direct quote, and connects each theme to your literature review. Aim for one to two pages per major theme.

Researcher Reflexivity

Reflexivity means being honest about how your own background, assumptions, and position as a researcher may have influenced your analysis. In interpretivist research, the researcher is not a neutral instrument: you chose what to code, how to label it, and which patterns to highlight. This is not a weakness; it is intrinsic to qualitative inquiry. But it must be acknowledged.

In your methodology chapter, include a short reflexivity statement (one paragraph is sufficient) covering: your role in relation to the research topic, any prior knowledge or assumptions you brought to the analysis, and how you tried to remain open to the data rather than confirming preconceptions. This directly addresses the confirmability criterion from Lincoln and Guba (1985) discussed in Section 3.

✓
Example reflexivity statement: "As a practitioner with prior experience in [relevant field], I approached the analysis with an awareness that my familiarity with the topic may have predisposed me to certain interpretations. To mitigate this, I conducted an initial immersive reading without pre-assigning codes, reviewed all AI-assisted outputs critically against the source transcripts, and invited peer debriefing from my supervisor on a subset of the coding."

Acknowledging the Role of AI in Your Analysis

Your methodology chapter should be transparent about the AI-assisted process. A recommended sentence is: "Initial code extraction was conducted using [AI tool name] as an analytical aid; all codes were subsequently reviewed, corrected where necessary, and validated by the researcher before being entered into the final codebook and coding matrix." This is honest, defensible, and reflects current best practice for AI-assisted qualitative research.
